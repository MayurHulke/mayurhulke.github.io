My love for robots started at the movies. First *I, Robot*, then *Big Hero 6*, and then Andy Weir's *Project Hail Mary* (I read the book before the film, and yes, that little guy up in the corner is Rocky). I wanted to build the machines I kept watching on screen, so I started entering robotics competitions and ended up winning **30+ of them at a national level** 🏆. That was the point I knew I'd happily do this for the rest of my life, one way or another.

So I went to learn it properly. I studied at the **University of Bristol** and spent my time at the [**Bristol Robotics Laboratory**](https://www.bristolroboticslab.com/), one of the biggest robotics labs in the UK. While I was there I started a venture building **intelligent bionic legs** for people living with lower-limb amputation, working alongside Paralympic athletes on high-performance prosthetics that could keep up with them. That's where I learned that the best robotics quietly gives someone a piece of their life back.

The thread through all of it, for me, has been building things that are actually useful. I've got nothing against the fancy demos, they're genuinely cool and they remind you what's possible, but we still don't see robots deployed at scale out in the world, quietly solving the hard problems they could be solving. They're just not there yet. That gap is the thing that keeps me pushing.

So I went after vision next, because it comes down to how robots actually *see* and understand the world. Vision is our dominant sense, with up to half of the brain's neural resources tied to visual processing, so it felt like the right place to start. I built a **7B vision-language model** for **spatio-temporal action segmentation**: show it a video and it breaks down what's happening, action by action, from short to long horizons. We deployed it across **60+ factory sites** for manufacturing giants like **ABB**, surfacing insights about the factory floor that simply weren't visible before, a real step toward the AI-driven factory. That data became the foundation for understanding and optimising how those processes actually run.

And that's the perfect segue back into robotics. Now that we can understand what's happening on the floor, I'm back to building robots that act on it, as **Robotics Lead at Almetra**, working on the **physical-intelligence layer** and automating some of the most complex, **dexterous manipulation** tasks on real hardware.

What pulls me in most is the learning itself: watching a robot pick up a behaviour rather than being hand-coded for every step. I've worked both sides of that line, classical control where the physics is well understood, like impedance control at 1 kHz, and end-to-end **learned policies** like diffusion policies, VLAs, and world models, and I'm convinced the best answers sit somewhere in between. I lean towards the world-models camp: the idea that real intelligence comes from a model that understands how the physical world actually behaves, not just from predicting the next token. Honestly though, it's still a green field. Nobody really knows the recipe yet for deploying robots at scale that can do genuinely useful work, and that's exactly what makes it worth chasing. Robots are where these ideas finally get tested for real, and the question I keep coming back to is simple: what actually works at scale? Because if it doesn't scale, it doesn't matter.

I'm building it from the ground up alongside the [**Google DeepMind**](https://blog.google/topics/google-europe/powering-the-future-of-robotics-in-europe/?utm_source=linkedin&utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=&utm_content=) robotics team, [**AWS**](https://www.massrobotics.org/massrobotics-aws-and-nvidia-announce-second-cohort-of-physical-ai-fellowship/), and [**NVIDIA**](https://www.massrobotics.org/massrobotics-aws-and-nvidia-announce-second-cohort-of-physical-ai-fellowship/), and I write down some of my points of view on robotics along the way in [my blog](https://letstalkwithrobots.substack.com/?utm_campaign=profile_chips), for anyone who loves building robots as much as I do. 👨🏽‍💻
